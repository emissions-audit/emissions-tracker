# Case Studies: When Self-Reported Emissions Don't Match Reality

Independent measurements from satellites, academic research, and investigative analyses consistently reveal significant gaps between what energy companies report and what they actually emit. These case studies illustrate why cross-validation of emissions data matters.

---

## Case Study 1

### TotalEnergies: The 3.6x Gap That Went to Court

**Self-reported**: TotalEnergies disclosed 455 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent for 2019 in its annual sustainability reporting, using the GHG Protocol's methodology with adjustments to avoid double-counting along its integrated value chain.

**Independent estimate**: In November 2022, Greenpeace France and climate consulting firm Factor-X published a report titled "TotalEnergies' Carbon Footprint: Accounts Are Not Good," estimating the company's actual 2019 emissions at approximately 1.637 billion tonnes of CO2 equivalent -- nearly four times the company's own figure. The independent analysis included Scope 3 emissions from sold products and investments that TotalEnergies had excluded or minimized.

**Discrepancy**: 1,182 million tonnes of CO2e, or roughly 3.6 times higher than reported. The gap stems primarily from how TotalEnergies accounted for (or excluded) downstream use-of-product emissions across its integrated operations.

**Why it matters**: This case became a legal landmark. TotalEnergies sued Greenpeace for defamation, but in October 2025, the Paris Judicial Tribunal ruled that TotalEnergies had engaged in misleading environmental advertising. The court ordered cessation of the claims and publication of the judgment on TotalEnergies' website for 180 days -- the first time a major oil company was held legally accountable for greenwashing its climate strategy.

## Case Study 2

### Saudi Aramco: The World's Largest Emitter With Half Its Footprint Missing

**Self-reported**: In its 2019 disclosures, Saudi Aramco reported Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions only from wholly owned assets located inside Saudi Arabia. The company did not disclose Scope 3 emissions from the combustion of its sold products. Its reported operational emissions positioned Aramco as having one of the lowest carbon intensities per barrel in the industry.

**Independent estimate**: A Bloomberg Green analysis found that Aramco understated its direct operational emissions by as much as 50% by excluding joint ventures and facilities abroad. Including all such facilities could add up to 55 million metric tonnes of CO2 equivalent to its annual Scope 1 and 2 tally. Separately, Bloomberg Opinion estimated Aramco's unreported Scope 3 emissions at approximately 1.6 billion tonnes -- over 4% of total global emissions. ClientEarth's Greenwashing Files documented the company's systematic exclusion of downstream emissions from its disclosures.

**Discrepancy**: On operational emissions alone, up to 50% understatement by excluding foreign joint ventures and refineries. When Scope 3 is included, the undisclosed portion dwarfs the reported figure by an order of magnitude. Bloomberg calculated that multiple refineries and chemical complexes abroad could add 28 million tonnes based on Aramco's ownership share.

**Why it matters**: As the world's largest oil producer, Aramco's reporting gaps have outsized consequences for global emissions accounting. Investors who relied on Aramco's low-carbon-intensity marketing during its 2019 IPO were making decisions based on an incomplete picture. The company only began reporting emissions from operated assets outside Saudi Arabia in its 2020 annual report, and still does not disclose Scope 3 use-of-product emissions.

## Case Study 3

### BP in the Permian Basin: Satellite Flyovers Reveal a 10x Methane Gap

**Self-reported**: BP's global environmental disclosures positioned the company as having lower emissions per barrel than competitors like Chevron, Shell, and ConocoPhillips, according to data compiled by Bloomberg. The company reported methane intensity figures consistent with its climate commitments.

**Independent estimate**: In 2021, the Environmental Defense Fund commissioned Carbon Mapper -- a nonprofit affiliated with NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory -- to conduct aerial surveys of the Permian Basin using infrared spectrometers. Over 11 days in July and August, Carbon Mapper detected more than 900 methane plumes from tanks, compressors, and pipelines. Bloomberg's analysis of this data found that BP emitted approximately 500 grams of methane per barrel of oil equivalent produced in the Permian Basin. By comparison, ExxonMobil's figure was less than 50 grams per barrel -- a 10x difference between the two operators in the same basin.

**Discrepancy**: BP's measured methane emissions per barrel were roughly 10 times higher than ExxonMobil's in the same oil field, despite BP's corporate disclosures suggesting comparable or better performance. The EDF study covered wells responsible for about 84% of BP's Permian Basin production, giving it strong statistical coverage.

**Why it matters**: This case demonstrates that company-level sustainability reports can mask significant facility-level variation. An investor comparing BP and ExxonMobil based solely on corporate disclosures would reach the opposite conclusion from what satellite data showed for their Permian operations. BP dismissed the findings, stating that "using a limited data set to draw these conclusions about BP's overall Permian methane emissions is misleading."

## Case Study 4

### The Permian Basin (Industry-Wide): MethaneSAT Finds Emissions 4x Higher Than EPA Estimates

**Self-reported**: Under the EPA's Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program (GHGRP), oil and gas operators in the Permian Basin -- including ExxonMobil, Chevron, ConocoPhillips, Occidental Petroleum, EOG Resources, Devon Energy, and Diamondback Energy -- report methane emissions using EPA-approved calculation methodologies based on engineering estimates and emission factors for routine operations.

**Independent estimate**: MethaneSAT, a purpose-built methane-sensing satellite operated by EDF, observed the Permian Basin from March 2024 to June 2025. Its data, published in February 2026, showed a methane loss rate of 2.4% of total marketed gas across the basin -- four times higher than EPA's official estimates. The basin was emitting approximately 440 tonnes of methane per hour, equivalent to over $577 million of wasted gas annually. A separate 2020 study by Zhang et al. in *Science Advances*, using ESA's TROPOMI satellite, had earlier measured a 3.7% loss rate -- more than double ground-based estimates of 1.2 teragrams per year. An RMI analysis found that when super-emitting events were considered, measured methane intensities were on average over six times higher than reported in the GHGRP.

**Discrepancy**: 4x higher than EPA estimates (MethaneSAT, 2026); 2x higher than ground-based inventories (Zhang et al., 2020); 6x higher when accounting for super-emitter events (RMI). The industry-pledged target of 0.2% methane intensity (Oil and Gas Decarbonization Charter) is 12 times lower than what MethaneSAT actually observed.

**Why it matters**: In March 2026, Senator Sheldon Whitehouse launched a formal investigation into the discrepancy, requesting information from eight major Permian Basin operators. The systematic gap stems from EPA methodologies that assume routine operations with minimal leakage, missing intermittent super-emitter events that satellites can detect. This case demonstrates that the problem is not limited to individual bad actors -- it is structural, affecting the entire basin and likely other production regions worldwide.

## Case Study 5

### Climate TRACE vs. Global Oil & Gas: A Systemic 3x Undercount

**Self-reported**: Countries that are party to the UNFCCC submit national greenhouse gas inventories, which aggregate emissions reported by oil and gas producers within their borders. These inventories form the basis for tracking progress against Paris Agreement commitments. Individual companies report through mechanisms like the CDP (formerly Carbon Disclosure Project) and sustainability reports, using methodologies that rely heavily on emission factors and engineering calculations rather than direct measurement.

**Independent estimate**: Climate TRACE -- a coalition using AI, machine learning, and data from over 300 satellites -- has independently tracked emissions from more than 352 million assets globally since 2021. For the fourth consecutive year (as of 2024), Climate TRACE data show that oil and gas emissions from production and transport are approximately three times higher than what countries report to the UNFCCC. Of the 50 largest individual emission sources worldwide, 26 are oil and gas fields and their associated infrastructure. Only 9% of publicly traded oil and gas companies in the MSCI All Country World Index report Scope 3 emissions from their investments, and including those "missing investment emissions" would increase the carbon footprint of a portfolio of the top 20 oil and gas companies by 24%.

**Discrepancy**: A systemic 3x gap between self-reported and independently measured emissions across the global oil and gas sector, persistent across four years of measurement. The gap is driven primarily by unreported methane leakage, underestimated flaring emissions, and excluded Scope 3 categories.

**Why it matters**: This is not about one company cooking its books. The gap is structural: industry-standard reporting methodologies systematically undercount real-world emissions because they rely on theoretical emission factors rather than direct observation. As Climate TRACE's data demonstrates, satellite-based verification reveals what bottom-up inventories miss. This is the core rationale for cross-validation tools that compare self-reported data against independent measurements.

## Summary: The Pattern

| Case | Reported | Independent | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| TotalEnergies (2019) | 455 Mt CO2e | ~1,637 Mt CO2e | 3.6x |
| Saudi Aramco (2019 Scope 1+2) | Disclosed figure | Up to 50% higher with foreign JVs | ~1.5x operational; Scope 3 undisclosed |
| BP Permian (2021 methane) | Corporate avg. | 500 g CH4/bbl | 10x vs. peer in same basin |
| Permian Basin industry (2024-25) | EPA GHGRP | MethaneSAT: 2.4% loss rate | 4x EPA; 12x industry target |
| Global oil & gas (2021-2024) | UNFCCC inventories | Climate TRACE | ~3x |

The direction is always the same: independent measurements find more emissions than companies and governments report. The magnitudes range from 1.5x to 10x depending on the scope, geography, and measurement method. This consistent, one-directional pattern is what makes cross-validation not just useful, but essential.
